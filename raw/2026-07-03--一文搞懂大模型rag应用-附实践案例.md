The article is an introductory RAG guide for large-language-model applications. It frames RAG as a lower-cost alternative to post-training or supervised fine-tuning when a general model lacks domain-specific, private, offline, or fresh knowledge. It also treats RAG as a way to reduce hallucination risk and avoid uploading sensitive enterprise data into third-party training workflows.

The core architecture is described as retrieval plus generation: retrieve relevant knowledge from an indexed knowledge store, inject the retrieved context into the prompt, and let the LLM generate an answer grounded in that context.

The article splits a complete RAG workflow into two broad phases:

- Data preparation: data extraction/loading, cleaning or normalization, metadata capture, text chunking, embedding/vectorization, index construction, and storage.
- Application/runtime: receive the user query, retrieve or recall relevant knowledge, inject the retrieved context into the prompt, and generate the final answer with the LLM.

Important implementation notes extracted from the readable mirror:

- Text chunking should balance embedding model token limits with semantic completeness.
- Retrieval quality is strongly affected by embedding model choice; general models may be enough for common cases, while special domains may need fine-tuning or custom embedding models.
- Candidate vector/search stores mentioned include FAISS, ChromaDB, Elasticsearch, and Milvus; selection should consider scenario, hardware, and performance needs.
- Retrieval can combine similarity search and full-text retrieval to improve recall.
- RAG prompts typically include a task description, retrieved background knowledge, and the user's question or instruction.
- Prompt design remains empirical and usually needs iterative tuning against real model output.
- The practice case uses Baidu Baike quinoa data as private-domain knowledge and LangChain to build a simple knowledge-QA example.
